## We don't recruit contributors, we notice them.

One of the most common questions we get on the Community team is:

"How can I get involved?"

It's a surprisingly tricky question to answer. Not because there aren't opportunities, but because there isn't a single path into the community. Some people build. Others teach. Others answer questions, organize meetups, improve the product, or create content that helps others.

The better question is: _How do I become someone the community notices?_

Our answer is simple: create value for other developers.

Build something that solves a real problem. Answer a question you know the answer to. Write the tutorial you wish you'd had. Share what you've learned. Share your expertise. Help organize a meetup.

Show up and do that consistently, and we'll notice.

## Find Your Thing

The best contributors don't all look the same.

Some community members love to build. Others love to teach. Some enjoy digging into hard technical problems to help someone get unstuck.

The common thread isn't what they do, but that they consistently show up.

Find the thing you're naturally drawn to.

### Build

The best projects rarely start with "I want to contribute to Supabase." They start with "I wish this existed."

Our favourite builders find the intersection between Supabase and something they already know well (Next.js, Kubernetes, Flutter, Rust, AI agents) and scratch their own itch. In doing so, they often end up helping lots of other devs.

Build the thing you wish existed, then share it with the community. We recently worked with [Jumski](https://github.com/jumski) on a community-supported [OpenCode integration](https://github.com/supabase-community/opencode-supabase). He'd been building in the ecosystem for years, had deep experience in the area, and was an obvious person to work with when the opportunity came along.

### Help

We notice people who show up.

Every day, experienced community members answer questions on Discord, Reddit, GitHub Discussions, Stack Overflow, and social media. They help someone debug a query, explain how RLS works, or point a developer toward the right docs.

Many of our best helpers have deep expertise in Postgres or another part of the Supabase ecosystem. More importantly, they're generous with that knowledge.

If you enjoy helping other devs and rabbit-holing on tricky problems, start showing up. Pick a corner of the ecosystem you know well and become the person people can rely on. You'd be surprised how quickly you can become the go-to person for your niche.

### Bring People Together

We notice people who create communities.

Some contributors organize meetups. Others run workshops, speak at conferences, or host hackathons. They introduce new developers to Supabase and create places where people can learn from one another.

Every community is different, so we want to give organizers the freedom to build the events they think people will actually enjoy.

If you enjoy bringing developers together, we'd love to help you do that.

## What Happens When We Notice?

Community is a two-way relationship.

When someone consistently shows up, we try to create opportunities that help them do more of it. Sometimes that's as simple as amplifying a project or introducing them to the right people. Other times it's funding open source work, commissioning content, sponsoring a meetup, or doing a paid collaboration on a new project.

The opportunities depend on what kind of contributor you've become.

Builders might receive funding through Open Collective or be commissioned to build integrations and libraries. We regularly work with content creators, fund meetup organizers, and invite trusted helpers into the SupaSquad—a flexible, paid role that, for some members, has become a meaningful source of income.

There's no checklist for any of this. We simply look for people who consistently show up and think about how we can help them keep doing it.

## See it in action

We've seen several community members join the team over the years.

[Tomás](https://github.com/tomaspozo), one of the authors of this post, started by answering questions on Discord. He'd been using Supabase himself and noticed that many non-technical users were struggling with the same issues, so he started helping wherever he could. We watched him show up day after day, and after just a few weeks, we invited him to join the SupaSquad. Today he's a full-time member of the Community team.

[Kalleby](https://github.com/kallebysantos) was a C# developer who just discovered Supabase and started contributing PRs to the Edge Functions team. It became obvious very quickly that he was a valuable community member. He joined the SupaSquad for a bit before joining the Edge Functions team full-time.

We've also seen long-term community contributions from tireless folks like [Gary](https://github.com/GaryAustin1), [Andrew](https://github.com/silentworks), [Ollie](https://github.com/hallidayo), [Ibrahim](https://github.com/k1ngarthur), [Inder](https://github.com/singh-inder), [Carol](https://x.com/CarolMonroe), and [Florian](https://x.com/florianldt), all of whom became well known in the community simply by showing up consistently and helping others.

## Where Do I Start?

Start with the thing you're already interested in.

If you love Kubernetes, jump into [supabase-kubernetes](https://github.com/supabase-community/supabase-kubernetes) and see how you can help. If you're deep into Next.js, try building a new component for the [UI library](https://supabase.com/ui). If you love tricky problems, start jumping into threads on Discord. If you enjoy teaching, write the tutorial you wish you'd had.

Once you've been contributing for a while, consider applying to the SupaSquad. It's how we recognize people who have become trusted members of the community.

Though if you've been consistent, there's a good chance we'll reach out first!
